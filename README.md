This course offers a practical introduction to R and data analysis for social scientists in 2026.

Goals: to provide a broad overview of how to use R for political science research and to highlight the opportunities it offers to researchers, adopting a contemporary approach that integrates AI tools and situates R within a wider research ecosystem.

Prerequisites: The course is designed for students with no prior experience in R. No background in quantitative methods, statistical software, or programming skills is required.  While not mandatory, students are encouraged to bring their laptop with a working installation of RStudio.

Content: The course is divided into three parts (each 1 hour). The first part of the course introduces the fundamentals of R and RStudio, covering basic arithmetic and logical operators, key data structures (variables, vectors, dataframes), and how to create, modify, and filter them. The second part focuses on working with real-world data, using the 2022 Italian election results as a running example. Students will learn how to install and load packages, import datasets, compute descriptive statistics, create frequency tables, and perform linear regression analyses. The third part is dedicated to data visualization with ggplot2, introducing the package’s layered logic and covering the main chart types: scatterplots for continuous variables, histograms for univariate distributions, barplots for categorical variables, faceted histograms for comparing groups, and countplots for relationships between two categorical variables.

Acquired skills: At the end of the course, students will acquire an understanding of how to use R, its underlying logics, and how they can use it in their own research.
